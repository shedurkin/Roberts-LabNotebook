1. miRNA Biogenesis — Nuclear Step

1.1 The Microprocessor Complex

The Microprocessor is the core nuclear complex that cleaves primary miRNA transcripts (pri-miRNAs) into precursor miRNAs (pre-miRNAs). It is minimally composed of:

Protein Gene Function Contribution to miRNA Pathway Key References
DROSHA DROSHA (formerly RNASEN) Class 2 RNase III endonuclease; catalytic subunit that cleaves pri-miRNA at the base of the hairpin stem ~11 bp from the ssRNA-dsRNA junction, generating a ~70 nt pre-miRNA with a 2-nt 3′ overhang Gatekeeper of canonical miRNA biogenesis. Acts at the first committed step — without DROSHA cleavage, no canonical miRNA can be produced. DROSHA determines the 5′ and 3′ boundaries of the mature miRNA by its precise cleavage position, directly defining the miRNA seed sequence and hence target specificity. Loss of DROSHA abolishes virtually all canonical miRNA production, leading to embryonic lethality and impaired stem cell differentiation. The 2-nt 3′ overhang it generates is the recognition signal for the next step (Exportin-5 and Dicer), coupling nuclear and cytoplasmic processing. Lee et al. (2003) Nature 425:415–419; Han et al. (2004) Genes Dev. 18:3016–3027
DGCR8 DGCR8 (DiGeorge syndrome critical region 8; aka Pasha in Drosophila and C. elegans) dsRNA-binding protein cofactor; dimerizes and stabilizes DROSHA; recognizes the ssRNA-dsRNA junction of pri-miRNA via its heme-binding domain and dsRBDs; essential for proper positioning of DROSHA on pri-miRNA substrates Essential orientation factor for the Microprocessor. DGCR8 determines WHERE DROSHA cleaves by recognizing the junction between the single-stranded flanking RNA and the double-stranded stem of the pri-miRNA hairpin. Without DGCR8, DROSHA cannot accurately locate its cleavage site, leading to aberrant or absent pre-miRNA production. DGCR8 also stabilizes DROSHA protein (preventing its degradation) and heme binding to DGCR8 stimulates dimerization and processing activity. In an auto-regulatory feedback loop, DROSHA cleaves DGCR8 mRNA to control DGCR8 levels, ensuring homeostatic balance of Microprocessor output. Gregory et al. (2004) Nature 432:235–240; Han et al. (2004) Genes Dev. 18:3016–3027; Nguyen et al. (2015) Cell 161:1374–1387; Partin et al. (2020) Nat. Commun. 8:1737

Structural notes: The functional Microprocessor is a heterotrimer of one DROSHA and two DGCR8 molecules. DGCR8 dimerization is stimulated by heme binding. DROSHA contains two RNase III domains (RIIID1 and RIIID2) that form the catalytic center, a central domain (CED), an arginine/serine-rich (R/S-rich) domain, and C-terminal dsRBDs. DGCR8 contains an N-terminal heme-binding domain, dsRBDs, and a C-terminal tail (CTT) that stabilizes DROSHA. DROSHA and DGCR8 engage in an auto-regulatory feedback loop: DROSHA cleaves DGCR8 mRNA to control DGCR8 levels, while DGCR8 stabilizes DROSHA protein (Han et al. 2009 Cell 136:75–84).

2. miRNA Biogenesis — Nuclear Export

Protein Gene Function Contribution to miRNA Pathway Key References
Exportin-5 (XPO5) XPO5 Karyopherin-β family nuclear export receptor; recognizes the ~2-nt 3′ overhang and dsRNA stem of pre-miRNA; transports pre-miRNA from nucleus to cytoplasm in a RanGTP-dependent manner; also stabilizes pre-miRNA by protecting from nuclear degradation Essential nuclear-cytoplasmic shuttle for miRNA maturation. XPO5 is the sole export receptor for pre-miRNAs and bridges the nuclear and cytoplasmic steps of miRNA biogenesis. Without XPO5, pre-miRNAs accumulate in the nucleus and are degraded by the nuclear exosome, causing a global collapse of mature miRNA levels. XPO5 also acts as a quality control checkpoint — it selectively exports RNAs with the correct 2-nt 3′ overhang and stem length, ensuring that only properly processed DROSHA products reach DICER. XPO5 binding additionally stabilizes pre-miRNA by shielding it from nuclear nucleases. Lund et al. (2004) Science 303:95–98; Yi et al. (2003) Genes Dev. 17:3011–3016; Kim et al. (2024) Nat. Rev. Mol. Cell Biol. (re-evaluation of XPO5 roles)
RanGTP RAN GTPase; provides the energy and directionality for XPO5-mediated nuclear export Energy provider for directional pre-miRNA export. RanGTP hydrolysis drives the conformational change in XPO5 that releases pre-miRNA into the cytoplasm. The RanGTP gradient (high nuclear, low cytoplasmic) ensures unidirectional transport — without it, pre-miRNA export stalls and miRNA biogenesis fails at the nuclear-cytoplasmic boundary. Lund et al. (2004) Science 303:95–98

3. miRNA Biogenesis — Cytoplasmic Step

3.1 The Dicer Complex

Protein Gene Function Contribution to miRNA Pathway Key References
DICER DICER1 Class 3 RNase III endonuclease; cleaves pre-miRNA at the terminal loop to generate a ~22 nt miRNA duplex; acts as a “molecular ruler” measuring distance from the 5′/3′ ends of the pre-miRNA; contains a PAZ domain (binds 3′ overhang), two RNase III domains, a helicase domain, and a dsRBD Final processing enzyme that liberates the mature miRNA duplex. DICER performs the second and final endonucleolytic cleavage, converting the ~70 nt pre-miRNA hairpin into a ~22 nt miRNA duplex ready for RISC loading. DICER’s PAZ domain recognizes the 2-nt 3′ overhang left by DROSHA, and its RNase III domains cleave both strands at a measured distance from the ends — this “molecular ruler” mechanism ensures consistent miRNA length across the miRNA repertoire. DICER loss abolishes mature miRNA production from pre-miRNAs, causing developmental lethality, defective stem cell differentiation, and widespread derepression of miRNA target genes. DICER also partners with TRBP/AGO2 to form the RISC Loading Complex. Hutvágner et al. (2001) Science 293:834–838; Ketting et al. (2001) EMBO J. 20:4234–4243
TRBP (TAR RNA-binding protein) TARBP2 dsRNA-binding protein; cofactor of DICER; binds pre-miRNA and enhances DICER processing efficiency; contains three dsRBDs (two bind pre-miRNA, one interacts with DICER); part of the RISC Loading Complex (RLC) DICER cofactor that optimizes pre-miRNA cleavage and bridges DICER to AGO. TRBP positions the pre-miRNA substrate for efficient and accurate DICER cleavage by binding the dsRNA stem via its dsRBDs. Critically, TRBP also serves as the molecular bridge between DICER and AGO2 in the RISC Loading Complex, directly coupling pre-miRNA cleavage with strand selection and AGO loading — ensuring that the miRNA duplex is efficiently transferred to its effector protein without release into the cytoplasm. Loss of TRBP reduces DICER processing efficiency and impairs RLC assembly, leading to decreased mature miRNA levels. Chendrimada et al. (2005) Nature 436:740–744; Haase et al. (2005) EMBO J. 24:4089–4100
PACT (Protein activator of PKR) PRKRA dsRNA-binding protein; paralog of TRBP; associates with DICER to facilitate miRNA processing similarly to TRBP; contains dsRBDs Parallel DICER cofactor with partially redundant function. PACT enhances DICER processing of pre-miRNAs similarly to TRBP but can also function independently in specific contexts. Unlike TRBP, PACT also activates PKR (protein kinase R) in response to dsRNA, linking miRNA biogenesis to innate immune signaling. Loss of PACT reduces miRNA production in certain cell types and impairs the cellular response to viral infection. Lee et al. (2006) EMBO J. 25:522–532; Lee HY et al. (2013) Mol. Cell 51:735–747
Loqs-PB loquacious (in Drosophila) Drosophila homolog of TRBP/PACT; required for efficient pre-miRNA processing by Dicer-1 The invertebrate functional equivalent of TRBP/PACT. Loqs-PB is essential for Dicer-1 activity in flies; without it, pre-miRNA processing is severely impaired. Included here for comparative purposes when studying miRNA machinery in cnidarians/corals, where homologs of Loqs/TRBP/PACT are expected but may not yet be fully annotated. Jouravleva et al. (2022) Mol. Cell 82:4049–4063

4. miRNA Function — RISC and Effector Machinery

4.1 Core RISC Components

Protein Gene Function Contribution to miRNA Pathway Key References
AGO1 AGO1 Argonaute protein; loads miRNA guide strand; mediates translational repression and mRNA destabilization Effector protein that executes miRNA-guided gene silencing (non-slicing). AGO1 binds the mature miRNA guide strand and uses its sequence as a template to find complementary mRNA targets. Upon target recognition, AGO1 recruits GW182/TNRC6 and downstream effectors to repress translation and promote mRNA decay. Without AGO1, a significant fraction of miRNAs have no effector platform for silencing. AGO1 also stabilizes bound miRNAs — AGO loading protects miRNAs from degradation. Meister et al. (2004) Mol. Cell 15:185–197
AGO2 AGO2 Argonaute protein; the only human AGO with endonucleolytic (“slicer”) activity for perfectly complementary targets; core component of RISC; binds miRNA guide strand via MID (5′ phosphate recognition) and PAZ (3′ end recognition) domains The catalytic heart of miRNA-mediated silencing and the only AGO with slicer activity. AGO2 is the most studied Argonaute and performs two critical roles: (1) as the universal RISC effector, it uses the miRNA guide to find and bind target mRNAs, then recruits TNRC6 and deadenylase machinery for translational repression and mRNA decay; (2) uniquely among human AGOs, it can endonucleolytically cleave (“slice”) perfectly complementary targets (e.g., some viral RNAs or siRNA-directed cleavage). AGO2 also plays a role in miRNA biogenesis itself — it processes some noncanonical pre-miRNAs independently of DICER. Loss of AGO2 disrupts the majority of miRNA-mediated silencing and is embryonic lethal. Liu et al. (2004) Science 305:1437–1441; Rivas et al. (2005) Nat. Struct. Mol. Biol. 12:340–349
AGO3 AGO3 Argonaute protein; miRNA-mediated gene silencing; lacks slicer activity Effector for miRNA-guided silencing (non-slicing). AGO3 contributes to the overall pool of RISC complexes that execute miRNA-mediated translational repression and mRNA destabilization, but lacks the slicer activity of AGO2. Functional redundancy with AGO1 and AGO4 in standard miRNA silencing, but may have specialized roles in specific cell types or contexts. Hauptmann et al. (2005) RNA 11:1580–1590
AGO4 AGO4 Argonaute protein; miRNA-mediated gene silencing; lacks slicer activity Effector for miRNA-guided silencing (non-slicing). Similar to AGO1 and AGO3, AGO4 forms miRNA-loaded RISC complexes for target repression. AGO4 may have specialized nuclear functions, including roles in transcriptional gene silencing and heterochromatin formation in some contexts. Hauptmann et al. (2005) RNA 11:1580–1590

4.2 GW182/TNRC6 Scaffolding Proteins

Protein Gene Function Contribution to miRNA Pathway Key References
TNRC6A (GW182) TNRC6A Scaffolding protein; binds AGO proteins via Gly-Trp (GW) repeats; recruits deadenylase complexes; bridges AGO-miRNA complex to mRNA decay machinery; essential for miRNA-mediated gene silencing The critical bridge between miRNA-target recognition and mRNA destruction. TNRC6A is the essential scaffolding protein that converts AGO-miRNA binding from a simple recognition event into an active mRNA silencing event. When AGO-miRNA finds its target mRNA, TNRC6A is recruited via GW repeats and serves as a docking platform for: (1) CCR4-NOT deadenylase complex (initiating poly(A) tail removal), (2) PAN2-PAN3 (initial deadenylation), and (3) PABP (coordinating deadenylation with translation repression). Without TNRC6, AGO-miRNA can bind targets but cannot efficiently repress them — silencing is severely compromised. Liu et al. (2005) Nat. Cell Biol. 7:1261–1266; Behm-Ansmant et al. (2006) Genes Dev. 20:1885–1898
TNRC6B TNRC6B Paralog of TNRC6A; binds AGO proteins; recruits deadenylase machinery for mRNA silencing Redundant scaffolding bridge for miRNA silencing. TNRC6B performs the same scaffolding function as TNRC6A, providing functional redundancy. The three TNRC6 paralogs may have tissue-specific expression patterns or subtle differences in AGO binding preference, but all can independently mediate miRNA-directed mRNA decay and translational repression. Baillat & Shiekhattar (2009) Mol. Cell. Biol. 29:4144–4155
TNRC6C TNRC6C Paralog of TNRC6A; binds AGO proteins; can recapitulate biphasic deadenylation for mRNA decay Redundant scaffolding bridge for miRNA silencing. Same function as TNRC6A/B with demonstrated ability to independently drive the biphasic deadenylation (PAN2-PAN3 followed by CCR4-NOT) that commits miRNA-targeted mRNAs to decay. Chen et al. (2009) Nat. Struct. Mol. Biol. 16:1208–1214; Lazzaretti et al. (2009) Mol. Cell. Biol. 29:6379–6392

4.3 Deadenylation Machinery (Recruited by TNRC6)

Protein/Complex Gene(s) Function Contribution to miRNA Pathway Key References
CCR4-NOT complex CNOT1–CNOT11, CNOT7 (CAF1), CNOT6 (CCR4) Major deadenylase complex; recruited by TNRC6; removes poly(A) tail leading to mRNA destabilization; CAF1 (CNOT7/CNOT8) and CCR4 (CNOT6/CNOT6L) are the catalytic deadenylase subunits Primary executioner of miRNA-directed mRNA degradation. The CCR4-NOT complex is the major deadenylase recruited by TNRC6 and carries out the second (major) phase of poly(A) tail removal during miRNA-mediated mRNA decay. CCR4 and CAF1 are the catalytic subunits that progressively remove adenosine residues from the poly(A) tail. Deadenylation is the rate-limiting step in miRNA-mediated silencing — once the poly(A) tail is shortened, the mRNA becomes vulnerable to decapping and exonucleolytic degradation. CCR4-NOT also contributes to translational repression independent of deadenylation. Loss of CCR4-NOT severely impairs miRNA-mediated mRNA decay. Behm-Ansmant et al. (2006) Genes Dev. 20:1885–1898; Braun et al. (2013) Adv. Exp. Med. Biol. 768:147–163
PAN2–PAN3 complex PAN2, PAN3 Deadenylase complex; mediates the initial (first phase) poly(A) tail trimming; PAN2 is the catalytic subunit; PAN3 bridges to TNRC6 Initiator of miRNA-directed biphasic deadenylation. PAN2-PAN3 performs the first, rapid phase of poly(A) tail trimming before CCR4-NOT carries out the extensive removal. PAN3 directly interacts with TNRC6, linking the miRISC to the first deadenylation step. This biphasic mechanism (PAN2-PAN3 → CCR4-NOT) ensures efficient and processive poly(A) removal, accelerating mRNA decay beyond what would be achieved by either deadenylase alone. Chen et al. (2009) Nat. Struct. Mol. Biol. 16:1208–1214
PABP (Poly(A)-binding protein) PABPC1 Binds poly(A) tail; interacts with TNRC6 (via PAM2 motif); coordinates deadenylation Coordinator of deadenylation and translational repression. PABP bound to the poly(A) tail must be displaced or reconfigured for deadenylases to access the tail. TNRC6’s PAM2 motif interacts with PABP, displacing it from the poly(A) tail and simultaneously recruiting deadenylases. PABP is also a positive regulator of translation; its displacement by TNRC6 contributes directly to translational repression. Thus, PABP serves as a nexus connecting miRNA-directed deadenylation and translation inhibition. Zekri et al. (2013) Mol. Cell. Biol. 33:22–34
PARN (Poly(A)-specific ribonuclease) PARN Deadenylase; involved in context-specific miRISC-mediated decay (e.g., miR-125b-AGO2 RISC) Context-specific deadenylase for select miRISC complexes. PARN provides an alternative deadenylation route in specific miRISC contexts (e.g., miR-125b-AGO2 RISC), expanding the repertoire of deadenylation mechanisms available to miRNA-mediated silencing. Its role appears more specialized than the CCR4-NOT/PAN2-PAN3 axis. Narasimhan et al. (2025) Noncoding RNA 11(4):62

4.4 Decapping & 5′–3′ Decay Machinery

Protein/Complex Gene Function Contribution to miRNA Pathway Key References
DCP1–DCP2 DCP1A/B, DCP2 Decapping enzyme complex; removes the 5′ m7G cap after deadenylation, committing the mRNA to 5′→3′ exonucleolytic decay The irreversible commitment step in miRNA-mediated mRNA destruction. After deadenylation removes the poly(A) tail, DCP2 (the catalytic subunit) removes the 5′ m7G cap, making the mRNA irreversibly committed to degradation. DCP1 stimulates DCP2 activity. Decapping is the critical “point of no return” — once the cap is removed, the mRNA cannot be rescued by re-polyadenylation. This step is downstream of and dependent upon miRNA-directed deadenylation. Chen et al. (2009) Nat. Struct. Mol. Biol. 16:1208–1214; Wang et al. (2002) Proc. Natl Acad. Sci. USA 99:12663–12668
EDC3, EDC4 (Hedls) EDC3, EDC4 Enhancers of decapping; facilitate DCP1–DCP2 activity Decapping enhancers that accelerate miRNA-mediated mRNA decay. EDC3 and EDC4 stimulate DCP2 decapping activity and help assemble mRNA decay complexes in P-bodies, ensuring efficient miRNA-directed mRNA turnover. Narasimhan et al. (2025) Noncoding RNA 11(4):62
XRN1 XRN1 5′→3′ exoribonuclease; degrades decapped mRNA; also degrades mature miRNAs in some contexts Terminal exonuclease that degrades miRNA-targeted mRNAs after decapping. XRN1 carries out the final degradation of the mRNA body after DCP2 removes the cap — this is the last step in miRNA-directed mRNA destruction. XRN1 also plays a role in mature miRNA turnover (degradation) in some contexts, providing a mechanism for regulating miRNA abundance independently of biogenesis. Chang et al. (2012) Nat. Struct. Mol. Biol. 19:572–579

4.5 RISC Loading Chaperones

Protein Gene Function Contribution to miRNA Pathway Key References
Hsc70/Hsp90 chaperone machinery HSPA8 (Hsc70), HSP90AA1/HSP90AB1 ATP-dependent chaperone machinery; required for loading of small RNA duplexes onto AGO proteins; facilitates conformational changes in AGO during RISC assembly Essential chaperones that open AGO for miRNA loading and activate RISC. AGO proteins in their apo (empty) state are in a “closed” conformation that cannot accept miRNA duplexes. Hsc70/Hsp90 use ATP hydrolysis to induce a conformational opening of AGO, allowing the miRNA duplex to enter. The chaperones then facilitate the retention of the guide strand and ejection of the passenger strand. Without this chaperone activity, RISC loading fails and miRNAs cannot be loaded into their effector proteins — silencing is abrogated even if miRNA duplexes are produced. Nakanishi (2016) Wiley Interdiscip. Rev. RNA 7:637–660; Iwasaki et al. (2010) Cell 140:672–680

4.6 Translational Repression

Protein Gene Function Contribution to miRNA Pathway Key References
DDX6 DDX6 (RCK/p54) DEAD-box RNA helicase; recruited by CCR4-NOT complex; promotes translational repression and P-body formation Effector of miRNA-mediated translational repression. DDX6 is recruited by the CCR4-NOT complex upon miRNA target recognition and directly inhibits translation initiation by interfering with eIF4F assembly and promoting 48S complex disassembly. DDX6 also promotes the aggregation of silenced mRNPs into P-bodies (processing bodies), where miRNA-targeted mRNAs are stored or degraded. This makes DDX6 a key executor of translational silencing — the earliest detectable effect of miRNA action, occurring before mRNA decay. Narasimhan et al. (2025) Noncoding RNA 11(4):62; Chen et al. (2014) Nat. Commun. 5:4722

5. miRNA Biogenesis — Accessory/Regulatory Proteins

These RNA-binding proteins (RBPs) modulate miRNA biogenesis by binding pri-miRNA or pre-miRNA terminal loops, altering structure, or interacting with the core processing machinery.

5.1 Microprocessor-Associated (Nuclear) Regulators

Protein Gene Function Effect Contribution to miRNA Pathway Key References
DDX5 (p68) DDX5 DEAD-box RNA helicase; Microprocessor auxiliary factor; facilitates pri-miRNA recognition and processing Promotes RNA helicase cofactor that broadens Microprocessor substrate range and efficiency. DDX5 associates with the Microprocessor and is required for efficient processing of a large subset of pri-miRNAs (94 of 266 examined in knockout studies). It likely remodels pri-miRNA secondary structure or resolves RNP complexes to make the cleavage site more accessible to DROSHA-DGCR8. Without DDX5, many pri-miRNAs are processed inefficiently, leading to reduced levels of a broad set of mature miRNAs. DDX5 thus acts as a global amplifier of Microprocessor activity. Gregory et al. (2004) Nature 432:235–240; Shiohama et al. (2007) Nucleic Acids Res. 35:5899–5911
DDX17 (p72) DDX17 DEAD-box RNA helicase; Microprocessor auxiliary factor; works cooperatively with p68; its sequestration by YAP downregulates Microprocessor activity Promotes RNA helicase cofactor that cooperates with DDX5 and links Microprocessor to signaling pathways. DDX17 works alongside DDX5 to promote pri-miRNA processing. Critically, DDX17 is sequestered by the Hippo pathway effector YAP in low cell-density conditions — this provides a direct signaling-Microprocessor link, allowing cellular context (e.g., cell density, proliferation status) to globally tune miRNA output. Without DDX5/DDX17, the Microprocessor processes many pri-miRNAs less efficiently, and this regulatory connection to signaling is lost. Gregory et al. (2004) Nature 432:235–240; Newman et al. (2010) Genes Dev. 24:1086–1092
hnRNP A1 HNRNPA1 Heterogeneous nuclear ribonucleoprotein; binds pri-miR-18a terminal loop and facilitates processing by altering pri-miRNA structure Promotes (context-dependent) Structure-remodeling RBP that enables processing of specific pri-miRNAs. hnRNP A1 binds the terminal loop of pri-miR-18a and induces a conformational change in the hairpin structure that facilitates DROSHA recognition and cleavage. This is a context-dependent requirement — moving the miR-18a hairpin to a different flanking sequence context eliminates the need for hnRNP A1, suggesting that hnRNP A1 overcomes inhibitory structural features in the native context. This demonstrates that individual miRNAs can have dedicated processing cofactors that overcome structural barriers, adding a layer of specificity to miRNA biogenesis regulation. Guil & Cáceres (2007) Nat. Struct. Mol. Biol. 14:701–707
KSRP (KH-type splicing regulatory protein) KHSRP KH-domain RBP; binds G-rich terminal loop sequences of a subset of pri/pre-miRNAs (let-7, miR-20, miR-26b, miR-106a, miR-21, miR-16); component of both Drosha and Dicer complexes; promotes recruitment of both Promotes Biphasic miRNA processing activator that operates at both nuclear and cytoplasmic steps. KSRP is unique among miRNA regulators in that it functions in BOTH the nucleus (promoting DROSHA processing) and the cytoplasm (promoting DICER processing). By binding the terminal loop of a specific subset of pri/pre-miRNAs, KSRP recruits the processing machinery and enhances cleavage efficiency at both steps. This dual-step promotion ensures robust production of a defined subset of miRNAs. KSRP antagonizes LIN28 binding to let-7 loops — where LIN28 blocks processing, KSRP promotes it — creating a competitive switch for let-7 biogenesis that determines stem cell vs. differentiated cell fates. Trabucchi et al. (2009) Nature 459:1010–1014
LIN28A / LIN28B LIN28A, LIN28B RNA-binding protein with cold-shock domain and zinc knuckles; selectively binds let-7 family pri/pre-miRNA terminal loops; blocks Drosha and Dicer processing; recruits TUT4/ZCCHC11 for pre-let-7 uridylation and degradation Inhibits (let-7 specific) Master developmental switch that blocks let-7 miRNA production to maintain pluripotency. LIN28 proteins are the most selective miRNA regulators known — they specifically target the let-7 family, which promotes differentiation. LIN28 acts at TWO steps: (1) in the nucleus, binding to pri-let-7 terminal loops to block DROSHA processing; (2) in the cytoplasm, binding pre-let-7 to block DICER processing AND recruiting TUT4/TUT7 to add oligo-U tails that mark pre-let-7 for DIS3L2-mediated degradation. This multi-step blockade ensures near-complete suppression of let-7 in stem cells. The LIN28/let-7 axis is a bistable switch governing the pluripotent-to-differentiated state transition — high LIN28 maintains stemness, high let-7 drives differentiation. Misregulation of this axis drives tumorigenesis. Viswanathan et al. (2008) Science 320:97–100; Newman et al. (2008) RNA 14:1539–1549; Heo et al. (2008) Mol. Cell 32:276–284
TDP-43 TARDBP DNA/RNA-binding protein; stimulates pri- and pre-miRNA processing for miR-132, miR-143, and others Promotes RBP that promotes a specific subset of miRNAs at both processing steps. TDP-43 enhances both DROSHA and DICER processing of a subset of miRNAs (miR-132, miR-143, and others) by binding their precursors. TDP-43 loss leads to reduced levels of these miRNAs, linking neurodegenerative disease-associated TDP-43 pathology to miRNA dysregulation. Kawahara & Mieda-Sato (2012) Nucleic Acids Res. 40:3415–3427
BRCA1 BRCA1 DNA-binding tumor suppressor; interacts with DROSHA; stimulates pri-miRNA processing Promotes Tumor suppressor that globally enhances pri-miRNA processing. BRCA1 physically interacts with DROSHA and promotes pri-miRNA processing, connecting DNA damage response and tumor suppression to miRNA biogenesis. Loss of BRCA1 leads to reduced mature miRNA levels, contributing to oncogenic derepression of miRNA targets. This provides a mechanism for the tumor suppressor function of BRCA1 beyond its DNA repair role. Kawai et al. (2014) Oncogene 33:3907–3917
EWSR1 EWSR1 RNA-binding protein; auxiliary component of the larger Drosha complex Facilitates Auxiliary factor of the extended Microprocessor complex. EWSR1 is part of the larger DROSHA-associated complex but its precise mechanistic contribution remains less well defined than DDX5/DDX17. It may assist in substrate recognition or in coupling transcription to pri-miRNA processing. Gregory et al. (2004) Nature 432:235–240
FUS FUS RNA-binding protein; auxiliary component of the larger Drosha complex Facilitates Auxiliary factor of the extended Microprocessor complex. Like EWSR1, FUS is part of the larger DROSHA-associated complex and may assist in substrate recognition or coupling of transcription with pri-miRNA processing. FUS mutations are linked to ALS, and miRNA biogenesis defects may contribute to disease pathology. Gregory et al. (2004) Nature 432:235–240

5.2 Cytoplasmic Regulators

Protein Gene Function Effect Contribution to miRNA Pathway Key References
TUT4/ZCCHC11 ZCCHC11 Terminal uridylyl transferase (TUTase); recruited by LIN28 to add oligo-U tail to pre-let-7, marking it for degradation by DIS3L2 Inhibits (let-7) Effector of LIN28-mediated let-7 degradation via oligo-uridylation. TUT4 is the enzymatic effector that carries out LIN28’s instructions to destroy let-7 precursors. When LIN28 binds pre-let-7, it recruits TUT4, which adds an oligo-U tail (3′ uridylation) to the pre-miRNA. This oligo-U tail is recognized by the exonuclease DIS3L2, which degrades the uridylated pre-let-7. 6. lncRNA Biogenesis — Transcription & Processing

lncRNAs share many biogenesis steps with mRNAs (Pol II transcription, 5′ capping, splicing, 3′ polyadenylation), but several features and processing pathways are distinct.

7. lncRNA Function — Chromatin-Interacting Machinery

This is where lncRNAs exert some of their most impactful epigenetic functions. Nuclear lncRNAs interact with chromatin-modifying complexes to direct their activity to specific genomic loci.

7.1 Polycomb Repressive Complex 2 (PRC2)

8. lncRNA Function — Post-Transcriptional & Cytoplasmic

8.1 lncRNA-miRNA Interactions (ceRNA / Sponge Mechanism)

lncRNAs can act as competing endogenous RNAs (ceRNAs) or “sponges” that sequester miRNAs away from their mRNA targets. The proteins involved are the same RISC machinery described in Section 4 (AGO, TNRC6, etc.), but the lncRNA provides additional binding sites.

Protein Function in lncRNA-miRNA crosstalk Contribution to lncRNA Pathway Key References
AGO1–4 miRNA-loaded AGO binds lncRNA ceRNA/sponge via miRNA seed pairing Effector that executes the ceRNA/sponge interaction between lncRNAs and miRNAs. AGO proteins mediate the physical interaction between miRNAs and lncRNA ceRNAs — the miRNA guide strand, loaded in AGO, base-pairs with complementary sites in the lncRNA just as it would with an mRNA target. When a lncRNA contains multiple miRNA binding sites, it can “soak up” multiple AGO-miRNA complexes, effectively depleting the pool of free miRNA available to repress its true mRNA targets. This ceRNA mechanism allows lncRNAs to indirectly upregulate genes that would otherwise be silenced by miRNAs, creating a post-transcriptional regulatory network. Singh et al. (2025) Mol. Biol. Rep. DOI:10.1007/s11033-025-10906-4
TNRC6A/B/C Scaffold for miRISC recruited to lncRNA targets Scaffolding that enables miRNA-mediated silencing on lncRNA ceRNA targets. TNRC6 proteins are recruited when AGO-miRNA binds lncRNA ceRNA sites, forming functional miRISC on the lncRNA. This means that the lncRNA ceRNA not only sequesters the miRNA but also engages the full silencing machinery (deadenylases, decapping enzymes), potentially leading to lncRNA degradation. The balance between miRNA sequestration (ceRNA function) and miRNA-directed lncRNA degradation determines whether the lncRNA acts as a sponge or as a miRNA target. Standard

9. Shared Machinery Between miRNA and lncRNA Pathways

Protein/Complex Role in miRNA Pathway Role in lncRNA Pathway Contribution to Both Pathways
DROSHA Cleaves pri-miRNAs Cleaves some lncRNA hairpins (e.g., DGCR8 mRNA, some lncRNA-derived miRNA precursors); noncanonical processing of lncRNAs Dual-function endonuclease at the apex of both miRNA and lncRNA processing. DROSHA’s primary role is cleaving pri-miRNAs, but it also processes certain lncRNA hairpin structures and regulates lncRNA levels through noncanonical cleavage. The Microprocessor can cleave hairpin structures within lncRNAs to produce miRNAs (as in miRNA-host lncRNAs) or to degrade the lncRNA itself. This creates a regulatory intersection: lncRNA levels can be controlled by DROSHA, and some lncRNAs are both miRNA precursors and functional lncRNA molecules.
DICER Cleaves pre-miRNAs Some lncRNA-derived small RNAs are Dicer-dependent; Dicer processes some lncRNA substrates Shared processing enzyme for small RNA production from both miRNA and lncRNA precursors. DICER processes pre-miRNAs into mature miRNA duplexes, but also processes certain lncRNA-derived hairpins that produce small RNAs. Some lncRNAs contain hairpin structures that are Dicer substrates, generating small RNAs with regulatory functions. This shared processing creates functional overlap between the miRNA and lncRNA pathways.
DGCR8 Cofactor for DROSHA Recognizes lncRNA secondary structures for Microprocessor; DGCR8 mRNA is itself a lncRNA-like substrate Junction protein that bridges miRNA processing and lncRNA regulation via the Microprocessor feedback loop. DGCR8 is essential for DROSHA activity in miRNA processing, but DGCR8 mRNA is itself a substrate for DROSHA cleavage (an auto-regulatory feedback loop). Since DGCR8 mRNA has lncRNA-like features, this feedback loop sits at the intersection of miRNA and lncRNA regulation.
RNA Pol II Transcribes pri-miRNAs and miRNA host genes Transcribes lncRNA genes The universal transcription engine for both pri-miRNAs and lncRNAs. Pol II transcribes both pri-miRNA genes (including miRNAs embedded in lncRNA host genes) and independent lncRNA genes. Distinct Pol II CTD phosphorylation patterns differentiate the transcription of miRNA host genes vs. lncRNA genes vs. mRNA genes, coupling transcription kinetics to downstream RNA processing fate.
Spliceosome Processes intronic miRNAs (mirtrons bypass this) Processes lncRNA introns Shared splicing machinery with divergent efficiency for miRNA hosts vs. lncRNAs. The spliceosome processes both miRNA host gene introns (which may contain miRNAs) and lncRNA introns. However, lncRNAs are typically spliced far less efficiently than mRNAs, which has consequences for both pathways: retained introns in lncRNAs contribute to nuclear retention, and mirtrons (intronic miRNAs that bypass Drosha) depend on the spliceosome for their biogenesis.
Exportin-5 Exports pre-miRNA Exports some lncRNA intermediates Shared nuclear export receptor with distinct substrate preferences. XPO5 primarily exports pre-miRNAs but can also export some structured lncRNA intermediates. The 2-nt 3′ overhang and stem length requirements for XPO5 recognition overlap with features found in certain lncRNA structures.
AGO proteins Core of miRISC Bind some lncRNAs; lncRNAs can be AGO targets (cleaved or repressed) Shared effector proteins with opposing consequences for miRNA vs. lncRNA function. AGO proteins are the core of miRISC (executing miRNA-mediated silencing), but they also bind lncRNAs as targets — lncRNAs with miRNA complementarity can be cleaved or repressed by AGO. Additionally, in the ceRNA mechanism, AGO-miRNA binding to lncRNA sponges depletes miRNA from mRNA targets, effectively converting AGO from a silencer to a vehicle for lncRNA-mediated gene activation.
KSRP Promotes Drosha and Dicer processing Regulates RNA stability including lncRNA Biphasic processing activator for miRNAs with emerging roles in lncRNA stability. KSRP promotes both nuclear and cytoplasmic miRNA processing steps, and also regulates the stability of select lncRNAs, suggesting broader roles in RNA metabolism that span both pathways.
LIN28 Blocks let-7 processing Interacts with lncRNAs in pluripotency networks Developmental switch protein that integrates let-7 miRNA suppression with lncRNA pluripotency networks. LIN28 specifically blocks let-7 miRNA biogenesis, but also interacts with lncRNAs in pluripotency networks, suggesting it may coordinate miRNA and lncRNA regulation during the stem cell-to-differentiated cell transition.
hnRNP proteins Regulate miRNA processing Bind and regulate lncRNA splicing, stability, and function Broad-spectrum RNA regulators that operate across both miRNA and lncRNA pathways. hnRNP proteins (A1, A2/B1, K, etc.) regulate miRNA processing (e.g., hnRNP A1 promotes pri-miR-18a processing; hnRNP A2/B1 interacts with DGCR8) and also regulate lncRNA splicing, stability, and function. Their broad RNA-binding activity makes them common mediators of cross-talk between the two pathways.
ADAR Edits miRNA precursors Edits lncRNA transcripts (e.g., in nuclear paraspeckles) Shared RNA editing enzyme that diversifies both miRNAs and lncRNAs. ADAR edits adenosine to inosine in both miRNA precursors (altering processing efficiency or target specificity) and lncRNAs (e.g., creating inosine-containing NEAT1 that is recognized by paraspeckle proteins). This shared editing creates regulatory diversity in both pathways.
Nuclear exosome Degrades aberrant miRNA intermediates Primary degradation pathway for many lncRNAs Shared quality control and regulatory degradation machinery. The nuclear exosome degrades aberrant miRNA processing intermediates and is the primary degradation pathway for most nascent lncRNAs. Exosome activity thus determines steady-state levels of both miRNA precursors and lncRNAs, making it a master regulator of non-coding RNA abundance.
CPSF/CstF 3′ processing of pri-miRNAs in some contexts 3′ processing of polyadenylated lncRNAs Shared 3′ end processing machinery that defines the 3′ termini of both pri-miRNAs and lncRNAs. CPSF/CstF process the 3′ ends of polyadenylated lncRNAs and also contribute to 3′ processing of some pri-miRNAs. Alternative polyadenylation choice (mediated by CPSF/CstF/CFIm) determines both lncRNA isoform identity and pri-miRNA processing efficiency.
